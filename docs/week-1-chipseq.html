Week 1: ChIPseq

0.8 Quality Control, Genome indexing and alignment

From the first project, you have knowledge of the initial steps that are shared by a wide variety of NGS experiments. Quality control is performed on the sequencing reads, an index is built for the species of interest and reads are aligned to the reference.

For RNAseq, it is important to use a splice-aware aligner when aligning against a reference genome since our sequences originated from mRNA. For ChIPseq experiments, our reads originated from DNA sequences and we can use a non-splice aware algorithm to map our reads to the reference genome.

Between project 1 and the early labs we did, you should have working modules that perform quality control using FASTQC and trimmomatic, build a genome index using bowtie2, and align reads to a genome. We are going to take advantage of the modularity of nextflow by simply copying these previous modules and incorporating them into this workflow.

  1. Copy the subsampled files from /projectnb/bf528/materials/project-2-chipseq/subsampled_files/ to a new directory called subsampled_files/ in your samples/ directory

  2. Encode the names of the file and the paths in a samplesheet at the top level of your directory

  3. Update your workflow main.nf to perform quality control using FastQC and trimmomatic, build a bowtie2 index for the human reference genome and align the reads to the reference genome.

0.9 Running multiqc to evaluate sequencing QC

Just like in project 1, we are going to use multiqc to collect the various quality control metrics from our pipeline so far.

  1. Make a channel in your workflow main.nf that collects all of the relevant QC outputs needed for multiqc.

  2. Copy your previous multiqc module and incorporate it into your workflow

0.10 Sorting and indexing the alignments

Many subsequent analyses on our BAM files will require them to be both sorted and indexed. Just like for large sequences in FASTA files, sorting and indexing the alignments will allow us to perform much more efficient operations on them.

  1. Create a module that will both sort and index your BAM files using Samtools. This will need to be a multi-line command as indexing can only occur on the fully sorted file.

0.11 Generating bigWig representations of our BAM files

Now that we have sorted and indexed our alignments, we are going to generate bigWig files or coverage tracks containing the number of reads per genomic interval or bin. We will use these coverage tracks for calculating correlation between our samples and visualizing the read coverage in specific regions of interest.
